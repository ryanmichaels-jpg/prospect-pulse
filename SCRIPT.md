# prospect-pulse, explained from the top

*How a GTM engineer would walk a peer through this project. Written as a reference for internalizing the framing — not a Loom narration.*

---

## 1 · The signals we believe indicate a company would benefit from Cursor

Before you score anything, you need a position on what good looks like. The position behind prospect-pulse is structural: Cursor's product disproportionately wins on a specific *shape* of engineering organization — codebases that lean TypeScript and React, monorepo-shaped repositories where multi-file edits compound, work that's frontend-bound enough that an IDE-and-Composer workflow beats a terminal-and-Claude-Code workflow, and a stack whose training distribution overlaps with the model Cursor is best at predicting against. That's the thesis the rubric is testing.

It runs against a popular alternative — "do they call themselves AI-native?" — which carries you for a while but starts losing signal by Q3 2026 when every B2B SaaS company puts "AI-native" on their careers page. Stack-shape doesn't expire like that. It's an architectural claim about which engineering cultures are structurally aligned to the product, and architectural claims don't get diluted by what the marketing team decides to call themselves.

In practice, the signals that flag a stack-shape match are TypeScript or JavaScript in a company's top three active languages by repo count (the active filter strips out vendored code and abandoned forks); the presence of a public NPM scope where packages cross-reference each other, which is what an internal monorepo culture looks like when it leaks out into open source; and corroborating server-framework dependencies — Next.js, Remix, Hono, tRPC — inside the package.json files of those repos. The countervailing signals — the disqualifiers — are JVM-language dominance in the top three (Java, Kotlin, Scala) and infrastructure-keyword density in the careers page that points at service-ownership models incompatible with the Composer-and-Tab workflow.

## 2 · How we pull them in (and what else we pull in)

The pull is from public sources only, because the test is whether an SDR can actually verify each claim on their own. Every signal in the rubric maps to an artifact a rep can click into and audit before sending an email. That's the design constraint, and it's the reason the rubric earns trust — there's no opaque model output and no proprietary data source between the score and the evidence.

There are four scanners. The GitHub scan reads a company's organization page through the API, walks the repository list, filters to active repos (non-fork, non-archived, pushed in the last twenty-four months), then counts languages, looks for migration keywords in repo names and descriptions, samples package.json files in the top five JS/TS repos for server-framework dependencies, and pulls contributor counts and commit velocity from the top-starred repos. The NPM scan walks the company's public scope through the registry API, counts packages, and most importantly checks whether any package depends on another package inside the same scope — that's the internal-cross-reference signal that separates a real packaging ecosystem from a one-off published SDK. The careers scan hits Greenhouse, Lever, or Ashby (whichever applicant tracking system the company uses) and looks for the platform-engineering, developer-experience, monorepo, and microservices keywords that corroborate stack-shape, plus the service-mesh and on-call-per-service phrases that corroborate the JVM disqualifier. The fourth pull is metadata — funding stage, employee headcount estimate, vertical, AI-native flag — entered manually in a YAML seeds file so the operator can override or contest a public signal when they have private information.

What this deliberately doesn't pull in: web crawls of the company's marketing site, scraped LinkedIn data, third-party data products like Apollo or ZoomInfo. The choice is principled — those sources either don't expose the inputs we care about, or expose them in a form that's adversarial to verify.

## 3 · How they are ranked

Each scanner emits structured results, and the scorer applies a weighted model. The model is deliberately not a black box. Every weight in `config.py` traces to an entry in `BETS.md`, the evidence ledger — so when a company scores 84 and another scores 94, both numbers decompose into a paragraph that an SDR can read and a sales lead can challenge.

The corroboration model is the part of the ranking that earns the rest of the rubric. A signal can fire at three levels: not at all, weakly, or strongly. Weak fires require one piece of evidence; strong fires require a second piece from a different source. TypeScript in a company's top three active languages is a weak fire, worth five points; TypeScript in the top three *plus* a Next.js dependency inside a repo's package.json is a strong fire, worth ten. The same shape applies to the NPM org footprint: a public scope with three or more packages is a weak fire, worth two; a scope where one package depends on another in the same scope (or names a design-system primitive like icons, tokens, or theme) is a strong fire, worth five. The corroboration ladder exists because single-source signals are mostly noise — a company can have a one-off TypeScript repo without being a TypeScript company, and a company can publish one SDK without operating an internal NPM monorepo culture.

Disqualifiers run on the same ladder in the opposite direction. JVM in the top three active languages is a weak fire and subtracts three; JVM dominance plus service-mesh or service-ownership corroboration is a strong fire and subtracts six. A full fire — strong corroboration plus a small engineering team — subtracts ten, because the deal-size economics that would offset the workflow penalty stop applying below a hundred engineers.

The total lands in one of four tiers — Tier 1 above 85, Tier 2 above 55, Tier 3 above 30, Tier 4 below — anchored to the score distribution of Cursor's own published customers. The validation gate is that no Cursor customer can drop below Tier 2 under the rubric, and the v2 design passes 8 of 11.

## 4 · What a rep would do with the output

The output is two artifacts: a ranked markdown digest and a CSV. The digest is human-shaped — a rep opens it Monday morning, sees their accounts ordered by tier and score, and reads the per-account rationale alongside the tier to understand *why* this account landed where it did. The rationale strings carry the actual evidence fragments — "strong ts_js_dominance [top 3 active langs: TypeScript (24), Go (8), Python (6)] · scribble-diffusion: Next.js in package.json" — so the rep doesn't have to take the score on faith. The CSV exists for CRM import, and carries the route name in a dedicated column so accounts can be filtered by motion.

The number is the prioritization input, but it isn't the operating instruction. The operating instruction is the routing pass. Every scored account is attached to one of five named outbound motions. Standard outbound is the default — lead with the strongest fired signal in the rationale. Split-pitch-JVM is for companies that fire the disqualifier *and* a strong NPM scope, meaning a frontend-platform team is publicly visible inside a JVM-dominant culture; the rep should lead with that team specifically and avoid the JVM service-ownership team. Split-pitch-infra is for pure-infra shops with a non-zero frontend surface — Cursor for the UI engineers, with the acknowledgement that Claude Code may be the better fit for the platform side. Individual-evaluator is for sub-ten-engineer teams where the right motion is PLG, not cold sequencing. And public-underestimates-internal is the explicit hatch for accounts where Cursor has a published customer quote but the public footprint is misleadingly thin — the rep leads with the quote and pursues the frontend platform team rather than chasing the public signal.

Each route carries a one- or two-sentence operational pitch plus a list of seats to target and seats to avoid. So when a rep looks at Databricks, they don't just see Tier 1 at 93; they see Tier 1 at 93, route `split_pitch_jvm`, target the frontend platform team, cite `@databricks/design-system`, avoid the JVM service-ownership team. The rubric tells the rep what to do, not just whether to do it.

The compounding effect — the part that matters for an SDR running a quota — is that every account in the digest now arrives with both a defensible prioritization decision and a defensible opener. That's the difference between a list and a tool.
